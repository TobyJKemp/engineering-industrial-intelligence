## One-Sentence Summary
Ontology versioning is the practice of managing and tracking changes to ontologies over time, ensuring consistency, traceability, and compatibility.

## Why This Matters to You
Versioning enables safe evolution of ontologies, supports collaboration, and prevents errors from untracked or incompatible changes. It is essential for regulated, collaborative, or long-lived knowledge systems.

## The Core Idea
### What It Is
Ontology versioning involves assigning version identifiers, documenting changes, and managing compatibility between versions. It may use tools, metadata, or repositories to track and control updates.

Versioning supports rollback, auditing, and impact analysis.

### What It Isn't
It is not just file naming or ad hoc updates. Effective versioning requires clear policies, documentation, and tools.

## How It Works
1. **Assign Versions:** Use unique identifiers for each release.
2. **Document Changes:** Maintain changelogs and metadata.
3. **Manage Compatibility:** Define policies for backward/forward compatibility and deprecation.

## Think of It Like This
Think of ontology versioning like software versioning: you track what changed, when, and why, so you can manage updates safely.

## The "So What?" Factor
**If you use this:**
- You enable safe, auditable evolution of ontologies.
- You support collaboration and compliance.
- You reduce risk of errors or incompatibility.

**If you don't:**
- Changes may be lost, undocumented, or break systems.
- Collaboration and integration are harder.
- Compliance and traceability are weakened.

## Practical Checklist
Before implementing, ask yourself:
- [ ] Are versioning policies and tools in place?
- [ ] Are changes documented and communicated?
- [ ] Is compatibility managed and tested?

## Watch Out For
⚠️ Inconsistent or unclear versioning can cause confusion or errors.
⚠️ Lack of documentation undermines traceability.
