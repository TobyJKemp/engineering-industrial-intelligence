## One-Sentence Summary
A traceability matrix is a table that maps and tracks the relationships between requirements, design elements, tests, and implementation artifacts to ensure completeness and accountability.

## Why This Matters to You
Traceability matrices help ensure that all requirements are addressed throughout the project lifecycle. They support impact analysis, validation, and compliance, and are especially important in regulated or safety-critical domains.

## The Core Idea
### What It Is
A traceability matrix links each requirement to its corresponding design, implementation, and test cases. It provides a clear audit trail and helps identify gaps or inconsistencies.

### What It Isn't
It is not just a checklist or a static document. A traceability matrix is a living artifact that should be updated as the project evolves.

## How It Works
1. **List Requirements:** Enumerate all requirements or objectives.
2. **Map Artifacts:** Link each requirement to design, code, and test cases.
3. **Review & Update:** Regularly review and update the matrix as changes occur.

## Think of It Like This
Think of a traceability matrix like a cross-reference table in a book: it shows exactly where each topic (requirement) is addressed throughout the text (project).

## The "So What?" Factor
**If you use this:**
- You ensure all requirements are implemented and tested.
- You simplify audits and compliance checks.
- You can quickly assess the impact of changes.

**If you don't:**
- Requirements may be missed or only partially addressed.
- Audits and validation become difficult.
- Change impact is hard to assess.

## Practical Checklist
Before implementing, ask yourself:
- [ ] Are all requirements and artifacts clearly listed?
- [ ] Are all links between requirements and artifacts documented?
- [ ] Is the matrix updated as the project evolves?

## Watch Out For
⚠️ Outdated matrices can give a false sense of completeness.
⚠️ Missing links may hide gaps in coverage.
