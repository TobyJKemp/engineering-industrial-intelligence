## One-Sentence Summary
An evidence graph is a structured, graphical representation of claims, arguments, and supporting evidence used to justify trust in a system or result.

## Why This Matters to You
Evidence graphs help you organize, visualize, and communicate the reasoning behind critical decisions or system assurances. They make it easier to audit, review, and defend claims—especially in regulated or high-stakes environments. Without evidence graphs, justifications may be opaque, fragmented, or hard to trace.

## The Core Idea
### What It Is
An evidence graph models the relationships between claims, arguments, and evidence as nodes and edges in a graph. Each node represents a statement or piece of evidence, and edges show how they support or relate to each other. This structure enables automated reasoning, traceability, and collaborative review.

Evidence graphs are often used in safety cases, compliance, and scientific research to make reasoning explicit and auditable.

### What It Isn't
An evidence graph is not just a list of evidence or a simple diagram. It’s a formal, machine-interpretable structure that supports logic-based reasoning and traceability. It’s not a substitute for strong evidence or sound arguments.

## How It Works
1. **Identify Claims:** Define the main claims or assurances to be justified.
2. **Map Arguments:** Structure supporting arguments and evidence as nodes and edges.
3. **Analyze & Review:** Use the graph to trace, audit, and communicate reasoning.

## Think of It Like This
Think of an evidence graph like a family tree for arguments—showing how each piece of evidence supports or relates to the main claim.

## The "So What?" Factor
**If you use this:**
- You make reasoning transparent and auditable.
- You support compliance and review processes.
- You enable automated reasoning and traceability.

**If you don't:**
- Justifications may be unclear or hard to defend.
- Auditing and compliance become more difficult.
- Errors or gaps in reasoning may go unnoticed.

## Practical Checklist
Before implementing, ask yourself:
- [ ] Are all claims and evidence clearly identified?
- [ ] Is the graph structure logical and traceable?
- [ ] Are tools in place for review and analysis?

## Watch Out For
⚠️ Missing or weak links can undermine the argument.
⚠️ Overly complex graphs may hinder understanding.
