## One-Sentence Summary
Functional Organizations group people by function (Sales, Engineering, Finance)—enabling specialization and efficiency but potentially creating silos and slowing cross-functional work.

## Why This Matters to You
Understanding functional structure helps you: (1) design organizations for efficiency, (2) recognize silo problems, (3) know when functional structure works and when it fails.

## The Core Idea
- **Specialization:** People grouped by discipline
- **Efficiency:** Leverage similar skills and knowledge
- **Standards:** Function sets practices and standards
- **Silos:** Potential for departments to work independently

## How It Works
1. **Organize by function:** Sales reports to VP Sales; Engineering to VP Engineering
2. **Specialize:** Deep expertise in function
3. **Establish standards:** Function sets best practices
4. **Cross-function coordination:** Coordinate across functions for projects

## Think of It Like This
Functional organizations are like **traditional law firms**: Partners organized by practice area (litigation, corporate); specialists within areas.

## The "So What?" Factor
- **Efficiency:** Specialization reduces costs
- **Expertise:** Deep knowledge in function
- **Standardization:** Consistent practices
- **Silos:** Functions may not cooperate

## Practical Checklist
- [ ] Is function-based work appropriate?
- [ ] Have I addressed silo risks?
- [ ] Is coordination designed?

## Watch Out For
⚠️ **Silos:** Functions become isolated; cross-function work difficult.
