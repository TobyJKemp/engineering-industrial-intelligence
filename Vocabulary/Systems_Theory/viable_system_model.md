# Viable System Model

## One-Sentence Summary
The Viable System Model (VSM) is a framework describing what any viable (able to survive and adapt) system must have—five interdependent functions managing complexity, learning, and adaptation necessary for long-term survival.

## Why This Matters to You
Organizations with AI agents must be viable—adapting to changes, managing complexity, learning. Understanding VSM helps you: (1) design organizations that actually survive, (2) diagnose why organizations fail (missing functions), (3) understand how adaptation and learning enable survival, (4) structure complex organizations effectively.

## The Core Idea

### What It Is
VSM defines five system levels:
1. **Operations (System 1):** Actual work units (departments, agents) doing work
2. **Coordination (System 2):** Manages coordination/interaction between operations
3. **Control (System 3):** Manages operations as whole; allocates resources
4. **Intelligence (System 4):** Scans environment; adapts strategy
5. **Policy (System 5):** Sets overall direction; balances internal/external

### What It Isn't
VSM is NOT:
- **Organizational chart:** Chart shows reporting; VSM shows functions needed
- **Rigid structure:** VSM functions exist; how they're organized varies
- **Complex theory:** Core principle straightforward; can apply at many levels

## How It Works

1. **Define System Boundaries:** What system are you examining?
   - What's inside? What's outside?
   - What defines viability? (What must system achieve?)
   - What's environment? (What's system responding to?)

2. **Identify Operations (System 1):** What units do actual work?
   - What are primary activities?
   - What are operational units?
   - What are their autonomy levels?
   - What are their objectives?

3. **Create Coordination (System 2):** How do operations coordinate?
   - What coordination mechanisms exist?
   - How do operations avoid conflict?
   - How is shared information handled?
   - What anti-oscillation mechanisms exist?

4. **Establish Control (System 3):** How is whole system managed?
   - Who allocates resources?
   - Who sets policies operations follow?
   - How are performance standards set?
   - How are exceptions handled?

5. **Implement Intelligence (System 4):** How does organization adapt?
   - Who scans environment?
   - Who identifies trends/opportunities/threats?
   - How is strategy adjusted?
   - How is learning captured?

6. **Set Policy (System 5):** Who sets overall direction?
   - What's organization's fundamental purpose?
   - How are conflicts between operations/environment handled?
   - What trade-offs are made?
   - What values drive decisions?

7. **Manage Information Flow:** Do all functions have information they need?
   - **System 3 monitor channels:** System 3 regularly checks operations
   - **Algedonic channels:** Exception reporting (problems escalate quickly)
   - **System 4 bridge:** System 4 brings environment into System 3
   - **System 5 authority:** System 5 provides coherence

## Think of It Like This
Viable System Model is like **organism's body:**
- **Operations (System 1):** Organs and cells doing work (heart pumps, lungs breathe)
- **Coordination (System 2):** Nervous system coordinating organs (heart and lungs coordinate)
- **Control (System 3):** Brain managing overall body (allocating energy, maintaining homeostasis)
- **Intelligence (System 4):** Sensory systems scanning environment (eyes, ears)
- **Policy (System 5):** Consciousness; overall direction (organism's goals)

Organism survives because all five systems working together.

## The "So What?" Factor

**With VSM alignment:**
- Viable (organization can adapt and survive)
- Complex environment handled (System 4 adapts to complexity)
- Effective operations (System 1 works well)
- Coordination smooth (System 2 prevents conflicts)
- Learning continuous (System 4 captures lessons)
- Clear direction (System 5 provides coherence)

**With VSM misalignment:**
- Not viable (organization can't adapt)
- Rigid (System 4 absent or weak; can't sense changes)
- Operations chaotic (System 1 not coordinated)
- Conflicts frequent (System 2 weak)
- No learning (System 4 missing)
- Incoherent direction (System 5 absent)

## Practical Checklist

Before using VSM:
- [ ] Have I defined system boundaries?
- [ ] Are System 1 operations clear?
- [ ] Does System 2 coordination exist?
- [ ] Is System 3 control in place?
- [ ] Does System 4 intelligence function?
- [ ] Is System 5 policy clear?
- [ ] Is information flow adequate?
- [ ] Can I identify gaps/misalignments?

## Watch Out For

⚠️ **Missing Function:** Each system necessary. Absence causes problems.

⚠️ **Misalignment:** Systems present but not well-integrated. Need information flow.

⚠️ **Over-Centralization:** Control and policy can dominate, suppressing operations/innovation.

⚠️ **Environmental Blindness:** Without System 4, organization can't adapt (fatal long-term).

⚠️ **Incoherence:** Without System 5, organization lacks direction (chaos).
