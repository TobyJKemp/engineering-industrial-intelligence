# Delegation

## One-Sentence Summary
Delegation is the process of assigning decision-making authority or tasks from one person (or agent) to another—transferring responsibility while maintaining accountability for results.

## Why This Matters to You
Without delegation, organizations bottleneck at the top. Everything gets decided by one person or group, creating slowness and limiting scale. With delegation, decision-making becomes distributed; more can happen simultaneously. In AI organizations, delegation becomes explicit: humans delegate decisions to agents, agents delegate to other agents, and everyone needs to understand who has authority to do what. Getting delegation right enables speed; getting it wrong creates chaos.

## The Core Idea

### What It Is
Delegation is distributing authority while maintaining accountability:
- **Authority transferred:** Person/agent receiving delegation gets power to make decisions
- **Responsibility:** Delegatee (who receives delegation) becomes responsible for execution
- **Accountability retained:** Delegator (who delegates) remains ultimately accountable for the outcome

Key principle: **You can delegate authority but not accountability.**

### What It Isn't
Delegation is NOT:
- **Abandonment:** Delegating and then checking in never = abandonment
- **Loss of accountability:** Delegator is still ultimately responsible for delegatee's performance
- **Unlimited authority:** Delegation comes with boundaries (budget limit, scope limit, approval requirements)
- **Permanent:** Delegation can be expanded, reduced, or revoked based on performance

## How It Works

1. **Assess Readiness:** Is the delegatee ready?
   - **Competence:** Do they have skills to execute?
   - **Trustworthiness:** Will they do the right thing?
   - **Time:** Do they have capacity?
   - **Track record:** Have they handled similar tasks?

2. **Define Scope:** What exactly is being delegated?
   - What decision or task?
   - What authority/resources?
   - What constraints/boundaries?
   - What success looks like?
   - Clear scope prevents misunderstanding

3. **Specify Constraints:** Delegation isn't blank check
   - Budget limits: "You can authorize spending up to $10K"
   - Approval requirements: "Decisions over $100K need my approval"
   - Escalation triggers: "Any customer complaint escalates to me"
   - Timeline: "You have 1 week to decide"

4. **Communicate Expectations:** Make clear what's being delegated
   - Explain why delegating (develops capability, distributes work)
   - Define success metrics (how will we know delegation worked?)
   - Establish feedback loop (how will delegatee get guidance?)
   - Make it formal so everyone knows about delegation

5. **Support Success:** Delegator enables delegatee to succeed
   - Provide resources needed
   - Make yourself available for questions
   - Don't hover, but do check in periodically
   - Offer guidance without micromanaging

6. **Monitor Results:** Track whether delegation is working
   - Are decisions being made well?
   - Are budgets/constraints being respected?
   - Is delegatee getting better over time?
   - Are there issues needing guidance?

7. **Provide Feedback:** Continuous learning from delegation
   - What went well? Do more of that
   - What could improve? Help them improve
   - If serious problems: intervention or revoke delegation
   - If consistent excellence: expand delegation

8. **Evolve Authority:** Adjust delegation based on performance
   - Successful performance → expand scope or authority
   - Problems → add constraints or revoke delegation
   - Growth over time → gradually increase responsibility

## Think of It Like This
Delegation is like **a parent teaching a child to ride a bike:**

- **Early stage:** Parent holds the bike steady (delegator maintains tight control)
- **Intermediate:** Parent holds bike lightly, ready to catch (delegator provides support, lets delegatee take responsibility)
- **Competent:** Parent jogs alongside (delegator monitors but delegates decisions)
- **Expert:** Parent watches from sideline (delegator checks in periodically but delegates fully)
- **Accountability:** Parent is still responsible—if child falls, it's parent's fault for not delegating appropriately

Delegation doesn't mean abandonment; it means progressively transferring authority as readiness increases.

## The "So What?" Factor

**If you delegate effectively:**
- Decisions happen faster (distributed decision-making)
- More happens simultaneously (not waiting for top approval)
- Delegatees grow in capability (delegation develops people/agents)
- Organization scales (more authority distributed, more capacity for work)
- Top leadership has time for strategy (not stuck in tactical decisions)

**If you don't delegate:**
- Everything bottlenecks at top (founder is the decision point)
- Organization doesn't scale (adding work requires adding layers of approval)
- People/agents don't develop (no opportunity to grow)
- Decision-making is slow (everything escalates)
- Burnout at top (too much responsibility)

## Practical Checklist

Before delegating, ask yourself:
- [ ] Is the delegatee ready? (Competent? Trustworthy? Capacity?)
- [ ] Is the scope clear? (What exactly are they deciding/doing?)
- [ ] Are constraints specified? (Budget? Authority? Escalation?)
- [ ] Have I communicated clearly?
- [ ] Do they understand success metrics?
- [ ] Have I provided necessary resources?
- [ ] Will I monitor appropriately? (Not hover, but not abandon)
- [ ] Will I provide feedback?
- [ ] Can I revoke delegation if problems arise?
- [ ] Will I adjust delegation based on performance?

## Watch Out For

⚠️ **Delegation to Unprepared:** Delegating to someone not ready sets them up for failure. Assess readiness.

⚠️ **Unclear Scope:** Vague delegation leads to misalignment. Be specific about what's being delegated.

⚠️ **No Constraints:** Unlimited delegation creates problems. Specify boundaries.

⚠️ **Abandonment:** Delegating and then never checking in leaves delegatee unsupported. Maintain some oversight.

⚠️ **Micromanagement:** Delegating but then controlling every decision defeats the purpose. Let delegatee own it.

⚠️ **No Feedback:** Delegation without feedback prevents learning. Tell delegatee how they're doing.

⚠️ **Accountability Confusion:** If delegatee thinks they're responsible for outcomes, but delegator thinks delegator is responsible, misalignment results. Be clear: delegatee is responsible for execution; delegator is accountable for ensuring delegatee does well.

## Quick Decision Guide

**Delegate when:**
- Someone else can do the work competently
- You're a bottleneck (decisions waiting for you)
- You want to develop someone's capability
- Work can happen with some constraints/oversight

**Don't delegate when:**
- Nobody else has competence to do it well
- Delegatee is not ready (lacks skills or trustworthiness)
- Too much oversight needed (would negate efficiency gains)
- Decision is rare or one-time
