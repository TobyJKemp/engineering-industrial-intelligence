## One-Sentence Summary
Decision Fields define the scope and boundaries of specific decision domains—clarifying what decisions fall within each field and who has authority in that field.

## The Core Idea
- **Bounded domains:** Decision authority bounded by field
- **Clarity:** Clear what decisions are in field
- **Authority:** Who decides in this field?
- **Overlaps:** Where fields overlap, how are conflicts resolved?

## How It Works
1. **Identify decisions:** What major decisions exist?
2. **Group into fields:** Which decisions belong together?
3. **Define fields:** What is scope of each field?
4. **Assign authority:** Who has authority in each field?
5. **Manage overlaps:** How to handle field overlaps?

## Think of It Like This
Decision fields are like **city departments**: Parks department decides about parks; public works about infrastructure; clear boundaries; when overlap, defined process.

## The "So What?" Factor
- **Clarity:** Clear authority in each field
- **Efficiency:** Decisions made in appropriate field
- **Accountability:** Clear accountability for decisions
- **Avoiding conflict:** Reduces authority conflicts

## Watch Out For
⚠️ **Field overlap:** Decisions that fit multiple fields.
⚠️ **Authority gaps:** Decisions that don't fit in any field.

## Practical Checklist

Before defining decision fields:
- [ ] Have you identified all decision areas in your organization?
- [ ] Are decision fields clearly defined and documented?
- [ ] Are there overlaps between fields?
- [ ] Are there gaps where decisions aren't covered?
- [ ] Is authority within each field clear?
- [ ] How do fields interact and coordinate?
- [ ] Can you add new fields as needs change?
- [ ] Is field structure communicated to organization?
- [ ] Are there exceptions or special cases?
- [ ] How do you handle cross-field decisions?
- [ ] Is there escalation path for field conflicts?
- [ ] How frequently do you review field definitions?

## Quick Decision Guide

**Define Decision Fields when:**
- Organization has multiple distinct decision areas
- You want clear ownership of decision domains
- Cross-field coordination is important
- Organization is growing

**Keep broad when:**
- Organization is small
- Decision areas are tightly coupled
- You prioritize speed over specialization
