# Authority Structures

## One-Sentence Summary
Authority Structures are the formal and informal patterns defining who has power to make certain decisions, direct others' actions, and control resources—establishing hierarchies of authority and decision rights throughout an organization.

## Why This Matters to You
Authority structure determines organizational speed and coherence. Centralized authority (one person/group decides everything) is slow but consistent. Distributed authority is fast but risks misalignment. Ambiguous authority creates conflict and paralysis—nobody wants to decide; everything gets debated. With AI agents, authority structures become explicitly designed—you must specify what agents decide autonomously, what requires human approval, what needs escalation. Getting this wrong creates chaos or bureaucratic bottlenecks.

## The Core Idea

### What It Is
Authority is the legitimate right to make decisions and direct action. Structures that organize authority enable organizations to function at scale.

Key dimensions:
- **Hierarchy Depth:** How many levels between frontline and CEO? (Deep = multiple layers; Flat = few layers)
- **Span of Control:** How many direct reports per manager? (Narrow = few; Wide = many)
- **Centralization:** Is authority concentrated at top or distributed? (Centralized = all decisions go up; Distributed = local decisions)
- **Decision Domains:** Which decisions fall under whose authority? (Financial decisions to CFO; customer decisions to VP Sales; technical to CTO)
- **Delegation:** Can authority be delegated? (Can a manager delegate to subordinates? Can agents delegate between themselves?)
- **Escalation:** When does authority escalate to higher level? (Budget over $1M? Strategic implications? Risk?)

### What It Isn't
Authority structures are NOT:
- **Org charts:** Org charts show reporting lines. Real authority structure includes informal power, expertise-based authority, and de facto decision-making.
- **One person's formal title:** Authority is distributed; the CEO doesn't have authority over everything despite what the title suggests.
- **Static:** Authority structures evolve with strategy, capabilities, and people. What worked last year may not work now.
- **Purely hierarchical:** Most organizations have both hierarchical authority (formal chain of command) and lateral authority (based on expertise or influence).

## How It Works

1. **Define Organizational Levels:** How many hierarchy layers make sense?
   - Few layers (flat): Fast decisions, local control, less management overhead. Risk: individual overload, inconsistency.
   - Many layers (deep): Clear authority, specialization of management functions. Risk: slow decisions, bottlenecks.
   - Most organizations have 4-7 levels from frontline to CEO
   - Choose depth based on complexity and control needs

2. **Determine Span of Control:** How many people per manager?
   - Narrow span (2-3 reports): Close supervision, management-intensive. Risk: managers become bottleneck.
   - Wide span (7-10+ reports): Hands-off management, less overhead. Risk: supervision quality suffers.
   - Optimal span varies by work type; technical work often needs narrower spans than routine work

3. **Distribute Decision Authority:** What decisions go where?
   - **Strategic decisions** (affects 5+ year direction): Board/CEO
   - **Operational decisions** (affects function/department direction): VP/Director
   - **Tactical decisions** (affects team operations): Manager
   - **Execution decisions** (frontline implementation): Individual/agent
   - Clear mapping prevents decisions from getting stuck

4. **Define Escalation Triggers:** When does authority escalate?
   - Decisions exceeding certain size: "Spending over $100K escalates to VP"
   - Decisions with cross-functional impact: "Changes to core systems require Architecture approval"
   - Decisions outside standard domain: "New customers in new markets escalate to CEO"
   - Risk-bearing decisions: "Safety-critical decisions require additional approval"
   - Clear escalation triggers prevent bottlenecks and enable speed

5. **Enable Delegation:** Can authority be delegated?
   - Some authority can be delegated (manager authorizes team lead to make day-to-day staffing decisions)
   - Some authority cannot be delegated (CEO's fiduciary responsibility; board authority)
   - Delegation enables speed but requires trust and monitoring
   - Clear policies on what can/cannot be delegated prevent confusion

6. **Implement Authority Clarity:** Make structure visible
   - RACI matrices (Responsible, Accountable, Consulted, Informed)
   - Decision authority guides ("Here's who decides what")
   - Clear communication that everyone understands
   - Regular review to verify actual practice matches intended structure

7. **Monitor & Adjust:** Does structure work as intended?
   - Are important decisions getting stuck?
   - Are people making decisions outside their authority?
   - Are too many decisions escalating (structure too narrow)?
   - Are too few decisions escalating (structure too loose)?
   - Adjust based on experience

## Think of It Like This
Authority structures are like **the command structure of a military:**

- **Centralized command:** General makes all decisions; everything flows up/down. Slow, but coordinated. Good for emergencies.
- **Distributed command:** Captains make tactical decisions; General makes strategy. Faster, but risk of misalignment. Good for normal operations.
- **Hierarchy depth:** Sergeant, Lieutenant, Captain, Major, Colonel—multiple levels of authority. Clear chain of command but slow.
- **Flat structure:** Sergeant coordinates directly with Captain; fewer layers. Faster but less specialization.
- **Span of control:** General commands 5 Colonels (narrow span) or 50 Sergeants (wide span). Narrow = close supervision; Wide = hands-off.
- **Delegation:** Captain can authorize Lieutenant to handle routine decisions; Captain must approve important decisions. Enables speed for routine work.

Military structures (often hierarchical/centralized) work for combat. Commercial organizations often use flatter, more distributed structures. Choose structure for your context.

## The "So What?" Factor

**If authority structure is clear:**
- Decisions happen at right speed (fast where speed matters; deliberate where thought matters)
- People know who decides what; no time wasted debating authority
- Accountability is clear (if decision goes wrong, you know who's responsible)
- New people get productive quickly (authority structure is transparent)
- Delegation can happen safely (clear what can/cannot be delegated)
- Organization scales (decision-making can be distributed without chaos)
- Agents can operate autonomously where appropriate

**If authority structure is ambiguous:**
- Important decisions get stuck (nobody wants to decide; everyone debates)
- Same decision gets made multiple times by different people
- Conflict over authority ("Who gets to decide this?")
- New people are confused about who they report to and who they listen to
- Delegation fails (unclear what people are authorized to decide)
- Organization becomes increasingly political (power determined by persuasiveness, not authority)
- Agents can't operate autonomously (unclear what they're authorized to do)

## Practical Checklist

Before finalizing your authority structure, ask yourself:
- [ ] Can I draw a clear org chart? (If not, structure is too ambiguous)
- [ ] For key decision types, can I say clearly who has authority? (If not, authority is ambiguous)
- [ ] Is the hierarchy depth appropriate for our work? (Too deep = slow; Too flat = overload)
- [ ] Is the span of control reasonable? (Too narrow = overhead; Too wide = poor supervision)
- [ ] Are escalation triggers clear? (When do decisions go up?)
- [ ] Can people explain authority structure to newcomers? (If not, it's too complicated)
- [ ] Does actual practice match org chart? (Informal authority often differs)
- [ ] Are important decisions getting stuck in authority ambiguity?
- [ ] Have we specified what agents are authorized to decide?

## Watch Out For

⚠️ **The Authority Vacuum:** Avoid situations where nobody has authority for important decisions. This creates paralysis and political maneuvering.

⚠️ **Authority Overload:** Concentrating too much authority in one person creates bottleneck and risk (if that person leaves or fails, organization is vulnerable).

⚠️ **Informal Authority vs. Formal:** Real authority often doesn't match org chart. Recognize and work with informal authority structures (senior technical people often have more influence than their title suggests).

⚠️ **Authority Creep:** People gradually assume authority beyond their designated domain. Clear boundaries help prevent this.

⚠️ **Authority Cascades:** Overly deep hierarchies create information delays and distortion as decisions cascade down ("telephone game" effect).

⚠️ **Authority Chaos:** Too many people with authority over same decisions creates conflict. Clear boundaries prevent this.

## Quick Decision Guide

**Clarify authority structure when:**
- Important decisions are getting stuck
- People are unclear who has authority for what
- You're deploying agents (must specify their authority)
- Organization is scaling and chaos is increasing

**Simple authority is fine when:**
- Organization is small (5-10 people; everybody knows how things work)
- Work is straightforward (few decision types)
- You're stable and not growing
