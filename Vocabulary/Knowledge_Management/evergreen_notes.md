## One-Sentence Summary
Evergreen Notes are concept-oriented, densely-linked, atomic knowledge units written in your own words that evolve and improve over time—designed not to capture fleeting thoughts but to develop lasting, interconnected understanding that compounds into a personal knowledge graph.

## Why This Matters to You
Most notes you write die on arrival. You capture a meeting, file it away, and never look at it again. You bookmark an article, write "interesting ideas here," and forget it exists. These are disposable notes—useful once, then dead. Evergreen Notes are different: they're living, developing understanding. An Evergreen Note about "API rate limiting" isn't a meeting transcript—it's your synthesized understanding of the concept, continuously refined as you learn more, linked to related concepts like "system resilience" and "user experience degradation," written clearly enough that future you (or your AI agent) can understand and build upon it. Over months and years, Evergreen Notes accumulate into a personal knowledge graph where each note strengthens others through connections. This transforms note-taking from documentation into thinking—externalized, compounding, increasingly valuable thought.

## The Core Idea
### What It Is
Evergreen Notes are a note-taking philosophy and practice developed by Andy Matuschak, designed to create durable, developing knowledge rather than ephemeral capture. The term "evergreen" comes from evergreen trees that stay green year-round—these notes maintain and grow their value over time rather than becoming outdated artifacts. Each Evergreen Note follows specific principles that make it fundamentally different from traditional notes.

The core principles of Evergreen Notes are: **Concept-oriented** (notes are about ideas, not events or projects), **Atomic** (one clear idea per note, not multiple concepts mashed together), **Densely linked** (each note connects to many related notes, creating a knowledge graph), **Written in your own words** (not quotes or copies, but your understanding), and **Continuously evolving** (notes are refined, expanded, and improved over time as understanding deepens). Together, these principles create notes that are discoverable, composable, and increasingly valuable.

In practice, an Evergreen Note might be titled "RAG systems trade retrieval precision for response accuracy" rather than "Meeting notes 2026-05-14." The content would be your synthesized understanding of this trade-off, not a transcript. It would link to notes on "vector search limitations," "context window constraints," "semantic chunking strategies," and "hallucination reduction techniques." Over months, as you learn more about RAG, you'd return to this note and refine it—adding nuances, updating examples, strengthening connections. The note "evergreens"—it stays relevant and grows more valuable.

For AI agent systems and modern knowledge work, Evergreen Notes create machine-readable, semantically rich knowledge graphs. Your AI agents can traverse these connections to understand relationships between concepts. When your agent needs to understand your architectural principles, it doesn't read meeting transcripts—it navigates your network of Evergreen Notes on architecture, design patterns, and trade-offs. The semantic connections between notes provide context that pure retrieval systems miss. Your personal understanding, captured in Evergreen Notes, becomes queryable institutional knowledge.

### What It Isn't
Evergreen Notes are not the same as regular note-taking or journaling. Traditional notes capture what happened (meeting minutes, lecture notes, project logs). Evergreen Notes capture what you understand about concepts. They're knowledge synthesis, not event documentation. You might create dozens of traditional notes that eventually inform one Evergreen Note that captures your synthesized understanding.

They're also not task management or to-do lists. Evergreen Notes are about concepts and understanding, not actions and deadlines. "Deploy authentication service by Friday" is a task. "Stateless authentication enables horizontal scaling but complicates session management" is an Evergreen Note. Tasks have completion; concepts have developing understanding.

Evergreen Notes aren't quotes or excerpts from sources. If you're copying article paragraphs into notes, you're not creating Evergreen Notes—you're collecting clippings. Evergreen Notes require processing: reading, understanding, synthesizing, and expressing ideas in your own words. This processing is where learning happens. The note is the product of thinking, not a substitute for it.

Finally, Evergreen Notes aren't static or perfect. The name "evergreen" might suggest they're finished, authoritative documents. Instead, they're living documents—"evergreen" means continuously valuable and alive, not frozen in final form. Your note on API design will evolve as you gain experience, encounter edge cases, and deepen understanding. Embracing revision and evolution is core to the practice.

## How It Works
Creating and maintaining Evergreen Notes follows a disciplined workflow:

1. **Capture Raw Input**: As you encounter information—reading, meetings, conversations, experiences—capture it quickly in whatever form is easiest. These are fleeting notes or literature notes in zettelkasten terminology. Don't worry about structure yet; just externalize the information.

2. **Process Into Understanding**: Regularly (daily or weekly), review your raw captures. Ask: What's the core concept here? What did I learn? How does this connect to what I already know? This processing transforms raw input into understanding. Don't just file the meeting notes—extract the concepts they contain.

3. **Create Atomic Notes**: For each distinct concept, create or update an Evergreen Note. Title it with a complete statement that expresses the core idea: "Microservices increase deployment flexibility but operational complexity" not just "Microservices." Keep the note focused on this single concept—if you're addressing multiple ideas, create multiple notes.

4. **Write in Your Own Words**: Don't copy-paste. Write the concept as you understand it, in language that makes sense to you. This forces comprehension and creates notes that future you (and your AI agents) can understand. Include examples from your experience when relevant—personal context makes concepts concrete.

5. **Link Densely**: As you write, identify related concepts. Every note should link to several others. "Microservices" connects to "distributed systems," "service boundaries," "API contracts," "deployment complexity," "organizational structure" (Conway's Law), and more. These links create the knowledge graph where understanding compounds.

6. **Refine Continuously**: Evergreen Notes aren't finished after first writing. Return to notes as you learn more. Add nuances, improve clarity, add links, challenge your earlier understanding. The best notes are revisited and refined many times. This is why they're "evergreen"—they stay relevant through continuous cultivation.

7. **Compose From Notes**: When you need to create something—documentation, articles, decisions, designs—compose by finding and combining relevant Evergreen Notes. Your notes become reusable building blocks for thinking and creating. This closes the loop: capture → process → create → capture improvements.

8. **Surface Patterns**: Over time, clusters of notes reveal patterns in your thinking. You might discover you have twenty notes about trade-offs, suggesting trade-off thinking is central to your understanding. These meta-patterns inform how you think and what you value.

## Think of It Like This
Imagine you're a gardener maintaining a garden that never has a season—it's always growing and producing (hence "evergreen"). You don't plant seeds randomly; each plant (note) has a clear purpose and location. You don't plant hundred-variety mega-plants; each plant focuses on producing one thing excellently (atomic). Plants aren't isolated; they form an ecosystem where each supports others through nutrient sharing and companion planting (dense linking). You tend your garden continuously—pruning, fertilizing, replanting, improving (continuous evolution). Over years, the garden becomes more productive and valuable, not because you planted more, but because you cultivated what you had.

Now imagine this garden is digital and AI agents can traverse it. When they need to understand your approach to system design, they explore the interconnected ecosystem of concepts you've cultivated. They don't read chronological logs of what you did; they navigate your evolving understanding of how things work. That's Evergreen Notes—a cultivated knowledge garden that grows more valuable over time.

## The "So What?" Factor
**If you practice Evergreen Notes:**
- Understanding compounds over time as notes strengthen each other through links
- You develop deep, personal knowledge rather than collecting surface-level information
- Creating outputs (articles, docs, decisions) becomes faster by composing from existing notes
- AI agents can navigate your knowledge graph to understand your thinking and expertise
- Insights emerge from connections between notes that weren't obvious in isolation
- Knowledge survives project boundaries—concepts extracted from temporary projects become permanent assets
- Revisiting and refining notes reinforces learning and reveals gaps in understanding
- You build a personal knowledge infrastructure that increases in value over years

**If you don't:**
- Knowledge remains fragmented in chronological meeting notes and project folders
- Understanding stays shallow—you know you encountered ideas but can't articulate them
- Every new project starts from scratch without building on accumulated conceptual understanding
- AI agents can't access your thinking—they only see event logs and task lists
- Patterns in your thinking remain invisible because concepts aren't connected
- Knowledge is trapped in project silos—when the project ends, the learning evaporates
- Repetitive re-learning occurs because insights weren't captured and refined
- Note collection grows without increasing in utility—more notes, same comprehension

## Practical Checklist
Before considering your Evergreen Notes practice effective, ask yourself:
- [ ] Are your notes concept-oriented rather than event-documentation? (principle test)
- [ ] Does each note address one clear idea rather than multiple concepts? (atomicity test)
- [ ] Are notes written in your own words, not copied from sources? (understanding test)
- [ ] Does each note link to multiple related notes, forming a knowledge graph? (connectivity test)
- [ ] Do you regularly revisit and refine existing notes as understanding deepens? (evolution test)
- [ ] Can you compose new writing by combining and building on existing notes? (utility test)
- [ ] Are note titles complete, specific statements of concepts? (clarity test)
- [ ] Can AI agents traverse your note connections to understand relationships? (machine-readability test)

## Watch Out For
⚠️ **Perfectionism Paralysis**: Waiting until you fully understand a concept before creating a note. Start with rough understanding and refine over time. Evergreen Notes are iterative—"continuously evolving" is a core principle. Imperfect notes that improve are better than perfect notes that never get written.

⚠️ **Premature Permanence**: Writing notes as if they're final, authoritative documents. Evergreen Notes should feel revisable and improvable. If you're intimidated to edit a note because it seems "done," you've violated the "continuously evolving" principle.

⚠️ **Disconnected Islands**: Creating atomic notes but failing to link them. Atomicity without connectivity creates isolated fragments, not a knowledge graph. The value emerges from connections—each note should link to several others.

⚠️ **Event Note Disguise**: Titling notes like "Thoughts on RAG" (event-oriented) instead of "RAG retrieval precision trades against response accuracy" (concept-oriented). If your note title sounds like a journal entry, it's not an Evergreen Note—rethink it as a statement about concepts.

⚠️ **Collector's Fallacy**: Believing that capturing quotes and excerpts counts as Evergreen Notes. Copy-pasting isn't understanding. Evergreen Notes require processing—reading, thinking, synthesizing, and expressing in your own words. The work is in the processing, not the collecting.

⚠️ **Ignoring AI Accessibility**: Creating Evergreen Notes only humans can navigate. Use consistent structure, meaningful links, and semantic markup so AI agents can traverse your knowledge graph and extract understanding.

## Quick Decision Guide
**Use Evergreen Notes when you need to:** Build lasting, interconnected understanding, develop expertise over time, create personal knowledge infrastructure for AI agent access, support creative synthesis and writing, enable knowledge to compound across projects, transform information consumption into understanding development

**Skip Evergreen Notes when:** You only need event documentation (meeting minutes, project logs), you're capturing temporary tactical information that's useful once then discarded, you're in pure execution mode with no conceptual learning, you don't plan to revisit or build on the knowledge
