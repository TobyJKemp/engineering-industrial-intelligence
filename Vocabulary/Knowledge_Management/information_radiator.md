## The Core Idea
### What It Is
An information radiator is a display showing critical information in a highly visible, continuously accessible location that provides ambient awareness to observers without requiring active interaction. The term was coined by Alistair Cockburn in the early 2000s describing agile practices where project status was displayed on physical boards visible to entire team. The concept derives from earlier lean manufacturing practices using visual management (andon boards in Toyota factories showing production line status).

Information radiators have several defining characteristics:

**High Visibility** - Positioned prominently where target audience naturally sees them. Physical radiators: large displays mounted on walls in team areas, visible from desks and walking paths. Digital radiators: dashboards on dedicated monitors, persistent browser tabs, ambient displays. Not hidden in folders, buried in wikis, or requiring navigation to access. Visibility is design requirement—if people don't see it naturally, it's not radiating. Example: Model health dashboard on 55-inch monitor mounted on wall facing ML engineering team workspace—every team member sees it when looking up from desk, walking to meeting room, or entering area. Not dashboard requiring login, URL navigation, or specific action to view.

**Ambient Awareness** - Information absorbed through passive observation, not active seeking. Team members gain awareness simply by being present—glancing while working, noticing while walking by, seeing changes peripherally. Don't need to: remember to check, take time to query, or intentionally seek information. Information comes to them through environmental presence. Like weather visible through window—you know if it's raining without going outside to check. Example: Developer working on feature, glances at wall display between coding sessions, notices deployment queue has grown—awareness gained without deliberate checking, informs decision to review pending deployments during next break.

**Minimal Interaction Required** - Information consumed through viewing alone, not interaction. No clicking, filtering, drilling down—critical information visible at glance. May support optional interaction for details, but core information radiates without it. Contrast with analytical dashboards requiring exploration, filtering, and interaction to extract insights. Information radiators are read-only displays optimized for quick comprehension. Example: Training status board shows: 5 jobs running (green), 2 jobs queued (yellow), 1 job failed (red, with model name visible). Status clear at glance—no need to click for details unless addressing failed job.

**Real-Time or Near-Real-Time** - Information current enough to inform immediate decisions and actions. Updates frequently enough that displayed information reflects actual state. Staleness acceptable only in minutes/hours, not days/weeks. Outdated information provides false awareness—worse than no information. Example: Production model performance dashboard updates every 5 minutes—recent enough for operational decisions. Not daily batch update (by time you see yesterday's issue, it's old news) or weekly report (completely stale).

**Focus on Critical Information** - Displays only information important enough to warrant continuous visibility. Not comprehensive data dumps, but carefully curated critical metrics, status, and alerts. Too much information overwhelms—becomes noise, not signal. Information radiator answers: "What does team most need to know continuously?" Example: Model health radiator shows: accuracy for each production model, error rates, latency p99, and active alerts. Doesn't show: detailed hyperparameters, training data statistics, or historical trends—those available in analytical tools, not critical for ambient awareness.

**Designed for Specific Audience** - Tailored to particular group's information needs and expertise level. Engineering team radiator shows technical metrics (latency, error rates, resource usage). Executive radiator shows business metrics (models deployed, incidents resolved, value delivered). Different audiences need different information at different granularities. Example: ML engineering team radiator: model training progress, deployment status, infrastructure health, active incidents—technical operational information they can act on. Product leadership radiator: feature deployment status, user impact metrics, sprint progress—business information supporting their decisions.

In 2026 AI/ML environments, information radiators manifest across multiple contexts:

**Model Performance Radiators** - Displays showing production model health and behavior. Metrics include: accuracy/precision/recall (against acceptance thresholds), prediction volume (requests served), latency (response time percentiles), error rates (failures per thousand requests), drift indicators (data distribution shifts), bias metrics (fairness across demographic groups), and alert status (active issues). Layout typically: grid showing all production models, color-coded status (green/yellow/red), key metrics visible, drill-down for details. Purpose: immediate awareness of model health, rapid issue detection, shared understanding of system state. Example: Fraud detection dashboard shows 6 models, 5 green (performing as expected), 1 yellow (accuracy 91.8%, slightly below 92% threshold, investigation triggered but not critical yet). Team immediately aware of developing issue.

**Training and Experiment Tracking Radiators** - Displays showing ML development activity and progress. Information includes: active training jobs (which models training, progress percentage, estimated completion), queued jobs (awaiting resources), recent completions (finished training with accuracy achieved), failures (jobs that errored with brief reason), experiment results (leaderboard of recent experiments with key metrics), and resource utilization (GPU/CPU usage, available capacity). Purpose: coordinate training resources, understand experiment velocity, avoid conflicts, learn from results. Example: Training dashboard shows: 3 GPUs running (model names, 67%, 89%, 34% complete), 2 jobs queued (waiting for GPU), 5 recent completions (with final accuracy), and current week's experiment count (23 experiments, 15% improvement over baseline). Data scientists see resource availability, plan experiment timing, and track progress toward objectives.

**Deployment Pipeline Radiators** - Displays showing model deployment status and velocity. Information includes: models awaiting approval (in review queue), models in staging (validating before production), models deploying (currently rolling out), recent deployments (completed with status), failed deployments (with brief error), and deployment velocity (models per week, time in review, time in staging). Purpose: understand deployment bottlenecks, maintain velocity, ensure models don't languish in queue. Example: Deployment board shows: 4 models awaiting approval (1 waiting 3 days—longest in queue, flagged yellow), 2 in staging validation, 1 currently deploying (progress bar 60%), 8 deployed this week (on track for 10/week target), and average review time 2.1 days (target <3 days).

**Incident and Alert Radiators** - Displays showing active issues, alerts, and incident status. Information includes: active incidents (severity, affected systems, time open, assigned owner), recent alerts (warnings that may become incidents), incident trends (count over time), mean time to resolution (how quickly issues resolved), and on-call status (who's responsible currently). Purpose: immediate awareness of problems, coordination on responses, shared understanding of system health. Example: Incident board shows: 1 P1 incident (recommendation service latency spikes—affecting users, incident commander assigned, 45 minutes old), 3 P2 incidents (elevated error rates on non-critical services—tracked, assigned to respective teams), 5 recent alerts (investigating but not yet incidents), and this month's incident count (12 incidents, down from 18 last month—improvement trend visible).

**Data Pipeline Radiators** - Displays showing data flow health and quality. Information includes: pipeline status (which pipelines running, succeeded, failed), data freshness (last update time for critical datasets), data quality metrics (completeness, validity, accuracy scores), processing lag (time from data arrival to availability), and data volume (records processed). Purpose: ensure data pipelines feeding models are healthy, detect data issues before impacting models. Example: Data pipeline dashboard shows: 5 pipelines succeeded overnight (green checkmarks), 1 pipeline delayed (data source unavailable, yellow warning, retrying), feature store freshness (last updated 7 minutes ago, green), and data quality scores (95% complete, 99% valid—acceptable ranges).

**Team Velocity and Sprint Radiators** - Displays showing team progress, capacity, and work status. Information includes: sprint burndown (work remaining vs time), velocity (story points completed per sprint), work in progress (active tasks by status), blocked items (impediments requiring resolution), and upcoming releases (scheduled deployments). Purpose: coordinate team work, identify blockers, maintain transparency, align on priorities. Example: Sprint board shows: burndown chart (on track to complete sprint goal), current velocity (38 points, above 35 average), WIP (2 in progress, 5 in review, 3 done), 1 blocked item (awaiting API access, escalated to ops), and next release (Friday, 3 models deploying).

**Resource Utilization Radiators** - Displays showing computational resource usage and availability. Information includes: GPU utilization (percentage busy, available capacity), CPU usage, memory consumption, storage capacity, queue depth (jobs waiting for resources), and cost tracking (spend rate, budget utilization). Purpose: coordinate resource usage, prevent capacity exhaustion, optimize spending. Example: Resource dashboard shows: 8 GPUs, 7 running at 95%+ utilization (high usage, good efficiency), 1 idle (available for new work), 4 jobs queued (waiting for GPU), current month spend $47K (on track for $65K budget), and cost by team (engineering 60%, research 40%).

**Key Performance Indicator (KPI) Radiators** - Displays showing business and operational metrics stakeholders care about. Information includes: business impact metrics (revenue influenced by models, cost savings, user engagement), operational metrics (uptime, availability, model deployment velocity), quality metrics (model accuracy, precision, recall averages), and progress toward goals (OKR tracking, milestone completion). Audience: executives, product managers, business stakeholders wanting high-level understanding without technical depth. Example: Executive dashboard shows: 15 production models (3 deployed this month), 99.5% uptime (SLA: >99%), average model accuracy 92.8% (target: >90%), $2.3M estimated cost savings from fraud prevention (ROI tracking), and Q2 objective progress (deploy 5 new models—3 complete, 2 in progress).

The common thread: information radiators reduce communication overhead by making critical information continuously visible, enabling ambient awareness that coordinates team activity without explicit communication—like living in same office where you overhear conversations and see colleagues' activities, maintaining shared context naturally. For distributed teams and complex systems, deliberate information radiators recreate this ambient awareness digitally.

### What It Isn't
An information radiator is not a comprehensive analytical dashboard. Analytical dashboards support deep exploration—filtering, drilling down, comparing time periods, running ad-hoc queries. Information radiators show limited critical information at glance—deliberately simplified for ambient awareness. Both have value for different purposes: use analytical dashboards for investigation and analysis, use information radiators for continuous awareness. Don't conflate—radiator overloaded with information stops radiating (becomes noise), dashboard too simple lacks analytical depth.

Information radiators aren't status reports or documentation. Reports are: intentionally consumed (read when needed), comprehensive (detailed information), static snapshots (point-in-time), and require active seeking (must open, read). Radiators are: passively observed (seen incidentally), selective (critical information only), continuously updated (live state), and ambient (visible without seeking). Reports provide depth for understanding details, radiators provide breadth for maintaining awareness. Use both—radiators for continuous awareness, reports for deep understanding when needed.

Information radiators aren't always digital displays. While modern radiators are often digital dashboards, physical radiators remain valuable: sticky notes on walls (sprint boards), printed charts updated daily (burn-down graphs), whiteboards showing status (model deployment queue), or even ambient indicators (colored lights showing build status—green light: all models healthy, red light: issue requires attention). Physical radiators have advantages: visible without screens, no login required, tactile interaction for updates. Choose medium appropriate for audience and environment. Physical works well for co-located teams, digital necessary for distributed teams.

Finally, information radiators aren't notification systems. Notifications push information to individuals when specific events occur (alert emails, Slack messages, pages). Radiators pull information to locations where people naturally look, continuously showing state even when no events occur. Both serve coordination—notifications for important events requiring immediate attention, radiators for continuous awareness of overall state. Notifications are event-driven ("something happened requiring your attention"), radiators are state-driven ("here's current reality"). Use notifications for critical issues needing response, radiators for ambient understanding.

## How It Works
Implementing information radiators effectively:

1. **Identify Critical Information for Continuous Awareness**: Determine what information team needs to stay synchronized. Ask team: "What do you wish you knew continuously without having to check?" "What information, if known sooner, would enable better decisions?" "What causes surprises that continuous awareness would prevent?" Common answers for ML teams: model health (accuracy, errors), system status (pipelines, deployments), issue awareness (incidents, alerts), capacity (resources, bottlenecks), and velocity (progress, blockers). Avoid: comprehensive dumps (everything anyone might want—overwhelming) or nice-to-have information (interesting but not critical—noise). Focus on truly critical information warranting continuous visibility. Example: ML team identifies critical information: production model accuracy (drops require immediate attention), training job status (coordinate resources), deployment queue (manage bottlenecks), active incidents (coordinate responses), and GPU utilization (plan experiments).

2. **Define Target Audience and Viewing Context**: Understand who will use radiator and where/when they'll see it. Audience determines: information granularity (technical vs business), metric selection (what matters to them), and presentation style (how they interpret). Viewing context determines: display size (distant viewing requires larger text), update frequency (how current must information be), and interaction model (pure viewing vs occasional interaction). Example: ML engineering team radiator—audience: data scientists, ML engineers, operations; viewing context: large wall display visible from desks 10-15 feet away, glanced at multiple times per hour while working. Design implications: large fonts readable from distance, technical metrics (latency, error rates), real-time updates, primarily viewing (occasional clicks for incident details).

3. **Choose Appropriate Visualization**: Select visual representations optimizing for quick comprehension at glance. Effective radiator visualizations: status indicators (color-coded green/yellow/red for health), simple charts (line graphs for trends, bar charts for comparisons), numeric displays (large numbers showing key metrics), progress bars (completion percentage), and tables (grid of systems with status). Avoid: complex visualizations requiring study (3D charts, intricate networks), small text (unreadable from distance), excessive detail (buried key information), and interaction-dependent (must click to understand). Test: can viewer understand key information in <5 seconds from across room? Example: Model health radiator uses: grid layout (one cell per model), color-coded backgrounds (green=healthy, yellow=warning, red=critical), large metric text (accuracy as big number), small sparklines (trend over last 24 hours), and alert icons (visible indicators of active issues).

4. **Establish Update Frequency**: Determine how often information must refresh to remain useful. Balance: currency (information fresh enough to inform decisions) against update overhead (too frequent updates distract, increase system load). Consider: decision urgency (how quickly must team respond?), metric volatility (how rapidly does information change?), and human attention (how often do people actually look?). Common patterns: real-time (seconds)—critical production metrics, near-real-time (minutes)—operational metrics, periodic (hours)—less volatile metrics, on-demand (triggered by changes)—deployment status. Example: Production model dashboard updates every 5 minutes (balance between currency and distraction—metrics don't change so rapidly that second-by-second updates add value, but 5 minutes fresh enough for operational decisions).

5. **Design Information Hierarchy**: Structure display to prioritize most critical information visually. Use: size (larger = more important), position (top/center = most prominent), color (red = urgent attention required), and visual weight (bold, bright vs subtle, muted). Viewer's eye should naturally flow to most important information first. Test with actual users: "What's the first thing you see?" should match most critical information. Example: Incident radiator—P1 incidents (critical, affecting users) displayed at top in large red boxes, P2 incidents (important, not user-facing) in medium yellow boxes below, recent alerts (investigating) in small gray boxes at bottom. Eye naturally drawn to red boxes—as intended, those require most urgent awareness.

6. **Position for Maximum Visibility**: Place radiators where target audience naturally sees them throughout their work. For co-located teams: wall displays in team areas, visible from desks and common areas. For distributed teams: persistent browser tabs, dedicated displays, or ambient devices. Avoid: hidden locations (back corner, separate room), requiring special access (login, VPN), or competing for attention (shared display rotating through multiple dashboards—information not continuously visible). Example: ML team radiator—55-inch monitor mounted on wall facing team's desk area, positioned at standing eye level, visible to 12 team members from their desks without turning. Not: requiring opening dashboard URL (barrier to casual viewing), rotating among multiple displays (information not always visible), or requiring walking to conference room (friction reduces viewing frequency).

7. **Enable Drill-Down for Details While Keeping Ambient View Simple**: Radiator shows high-level critical information at glance, but supports optional interaction for details when needed. Primary view: comprehensive at-a-glance status, no interaction required. Secondary views: available on click/hover for investigation. Example: Model health radiator shows: model name, current accuracy (big number), status color (green/yellow/red), and alert count—all visible without interaction. Click on model: detailed dashboard showing accuracy over time, error breakdown by type, latency percentiles, recent predictions, and configuration—for investigation when needed. Key principle: radiating information doesn't require interaction, but details available when investigating.

8. **Establish Clear Ownership and Maintenance**: Designate responsibility for radiator upkeep. Owner responsibilities: ensure data pipelines feeding radiator remain operational, monitor radiator itself (is it displaying correctly?), evolve radiator based on feedback (what information should be added/removed?), and train team on interpretation (how to read radiator, what actions to take). Without ownership, radiators decay—data becomes stale, display breaks, nobody fixes it, team stops trusting/using it. Example: ML platform team owns model health radiator—maintains data collection pipeline, monitors dashboard uptime, quarterly reviews with ML teams for feedback ("what's useful? what's missing?"), and documents interpretation guidelines ("yellow = warning, investigate during business hours; red = critical, investigate immediately").

9. **Define Action Thresholds and Responses**: Establish clear criteria for when radiator information requires action. Specify: green status (healthy, no action needed), yellow status (warning, investigation during normal work hours), red status (critical, immediate investigation required), and escalation procedures (who to notify, how to escalate). Radiators without action thresholds become wallpaper—team sees information but doesn't know what to do with it. Example: Model accuracy radiator thresholds: Green (>92% accuracy, no action), Yellow (90-92% accuracy, data scientist investigates within 4 hours, assesses need for model refresh), Red (<90% accuracy, immediate investigation, potentially rollback to previous model version, incident created, on-call engineer paged). Thresholds transform awareness into coordinated response.

10. **Integrate with Existing Workflows**: Connect radiators to team's normal work patterns and tooling. Radiator shouldn't be isolated—should integrate with incident management (clicking red status creates incident), communication tools (alerts also post to Slack), ticketing systems (yellow statuses generate tickets), and documentation (links to runbooks for common issues). Integration makes radiator actionable—not just displaying information, but enabling efficient response. Example: When deployment radiator shows model stuck in approval queue >3 days (yellow), clicking opens approval workflow showing pending reviewers, sends Slack reminder to reviewers, and links to model documentation for review. Radiator makes issue visible, integration enables efficient resolution.

11. **Test Readability and Comprehension**: Validate radiator with actual users in actual environment. Tests: Distance reading (can you read key information from your desk?), quick comprehension (understand status in <5 seconds?), attention (do you naturally notice it?), actionability (do you know what to do when you see issues?), and trust (is information accurate and current?). Iterate based on feedback. Example testing: Team member stands at desk, glances at wall radiator—can they identify: which models are healthy vs problematic, whether any training jobs failed, if incidents are active? If requires squinting, longer study, or confusion, redesign before deploying.

12. **Avoid Information Overload**: Resist temptation to show everything. More information ≠ better radiator. Overloaded radiators become noise—team stops looking because too much to process. Guidelines: show <10 primary metrics (human can track 5-9 items in working memory), use whitespace (don't pack display edge-to-edge), focus on status over details (show "model healthy" not 47 detailed metrics), and refresh judiciously (constant motion distracts). If struggling to fit information, probably trying to show too much—split into multiple focused radiators or use hierarchical reveal (summary view, details on interaction). Example: Training status radiator shows only: active jobs count, queued jobs count, GPU utilization percentage, and failures today—four simple metrics providing essential awareness. Detailed training history, individual job parameters, resource allocation details—available in analytical dashboard, not radiating continuously.

13. **Iterate Based on Usage and Feedback**: Monitor how team uses radiator, collect feedback, evolve design. Monitor: view frequency (how often do people look?), interaction patterns (what do they click on?), response times (do issues get addressed faster?), and sentiment (do team members find it useful?). Collect feedback regularly: "What information is most useful? What's missing? What's unnecessary? Is anything confusing?" Evolve quarterly: add new metrics addressing gaps, remove unused information, refine thresholds based on experience, and improve visualizations. Example: Initial deployment radiator showed models awaiting approval and recently deployed. Feedback: "We can't tell who's blocking approvals—need to see pending reviewers." Evolution: Added reviewer names to pending approvals. Impact: 30% faster approval time as visibility created accountability. Next evolution: Add typical review time for each reviewer to set expectations.

14. **Educate Team on Interpretation and Response**: Ensure team understands what radiator shows and how to respond. Education includes: onboarding (new team members learn radiator during onboarding), documentation (written guide to radiator interpretation), examples (scenarios showing green/yellow/red states and appropriate responses), and periodic refreshers (as radiator evolves or new team members join). Don't assume radiator is self-explanatory. Example: Model health radiator training covers: what each metric means (accuracy, latency, error rate), what thresholds trigger yellow/red status, typical causes of degradation (data drift, infrastructure issues, upstream changes), initial troubleshooting steps (check recent deployments, review error logs, verify data pipeline), and escalation procedure (create incident, notify on-call, join war room Slack channel). Training transforms radiator from passive display to active coordination tool.

## Think of It Like This
Imagine driving a car. Dashboard radiates critical information continuously while you drive: speedometer (current speed), fuel gauge (remaining fuel), engine temperature, warning lights (check engine, low oil, low tire pressure). You don't actively query car for information—don't ask "What's my speed?" Instead, information radiates from dashboard—you glance down momentarily, see speed, return attention to road. Dashboard is designed for ambient awareness: large clear numbers, color-coded warnings (red = critical), positioned in natural sight line, real-time or near-real-time, showing only critical information (not comprehensive diagnostic data—that's for mechanics, not drivers).

Imagine dashboard showed comprehensive engine data: piston temperature, combustion efficiency, transmission gear ratios, electronic control unit parameters—50 metrics covering entire dashboard. You'd be overwhelmed, unable to extract critical information (am I speeding? do I need gas?). Or imagine dashboard required interaction: must click button to see speed, navigate menu to check fuel. While driving? Dangerous and impractical. Or imagine dashboard updated daily: shows yesterday's speed, yesterday's fuel level. Useless for driving decisions.

Good dashboard radiates critical information continuously, clearly, with minimal attention required—enabling you to drive safely while maintaining awareness. Information radiators function identically in workplace. Without radiators: must actively query systems ("What's model accuracy today?"), navigate tools, run reports—consuming attention and time. With radiators: critical information visible continuously (model accuracy displayed, updated automatically), enabling you to work while maintaining ambient awareness. Glance at radiator like glancing at car dashboard—instant understanding without disrupting flow.

## Watch Out For
⚠️ **Information Overload**: Attempting to show too much information on single radiator. 50 metrics, dense tables, small text—overwhelming, not ambient. Team stops looking because requires too much mental effort to process. Solution: Ruthless prioritization—show only truly critical information. Guideline: <10 primary metrics, use whitespace liberally, focus on status over details. If need to show more, create multiple focused radiators (model health, training status, incident tracking—separate displays) rather than cramming everything into one. Test: can viewer extract key information in <5 seconds? If no, too much information.

⚠️ **Stale Information**: Radiator displaying outdated information creating false awareness—worse than no information. Team sees "all models green" but data is 2 hours old, missing recent accuracy drop. Team has false confidence leading to delayed response. Solution: Ensure update pipelines robust and monitored. Display "last updated" timestamp prominently—if old, team knows to verify before trusting. Alert if data feed fails (radiator itself should show "data connection lost"). For critical radiators, have automated health checks ensuring freshness. Principle: no information better than wrong information.

⚠️ **Dashboard as Radiator Confusion**: Using comprehensive analytical dashboard as information radiator. Dashboard has 20 charts, multiple tabs, requires interaction—not effective radiator. Or making radiator so detailed it becomes analytical tool, losing ambient awareness benefit. Solution: Clear distinction—analytical dashboards for investigation (detailed, interactive, comprehensive), information radiators for awareness (simple, passive, selective). May link from radiator to dashboard (click for details), but radiator itself stays simple. Don't try to make one tool serve both purposes—each optimized for different goals.

⚠️ **Poor Visibility and Positioning**: Radiator in location team doesn't naturally see. Requires walking to conference room (friction), hidden in corner of workspace (not in sight line), or only accessible via specific URL requiring login (barrier). Information not visible is not radiating. Solution: Deliberate positioning where target audience naturally looks during normal work. For co-located teams: prominent wall displays in team areas. For distributed teams: persistent browser tabs, dedicated displays on desks, or Slack integrations. Test: do team members actually see radiator naturally throughout their day without special effort?

⚠️ **Absence of Action Thresholds**: Radiator displays information but team doesn't know what to do with it. See model accuracy at 91%—is that good, bad, requires action? Confusion leads to either: ignoring radiator (wallpaper effect) or unnecessary alerts (overreaction). Solution: Define clear thresholds and responses. Document: green means X (no action), yellow means Y (investigate during business hours), red means Z (immediate response, page on-call). Make thresholds visible on radiator itself ("target >92%") so viewers can interpret status. Train team on appropriate responses to each status level.

⚠️ **Lack of Ownership**: Nobody responsible for maintaining radiator. Data pipeline breaks, radiator shows stale data for weeks, nobody notices or fixes. Display breaks, nobody repairs it. Team needs change, nobody updates radiator. Unmaintained radiators decay and become useless. Solution: Explicit ownership—designate individual or team responsible for radiator health. Owner maintains data pipelines, monitors radiator itself, evolves based on feedback, and trains team on interpretation. Include radiator maintenance in owner's formal responsibilities and performance expectations, not informal "someone should probably..." Ownership prevents decay.

⚠️ **Overwhelming Motion and Animation**: Radiator constantly animating, blinking, moving—intended to draw attention but actually creates distraction. Team can't work because peripheral vision constantly triggered by motion. Radiators become annoying rather than helpful, team positions desks to avoid seeing them. Solution: Minimize animation—use static displays updating smoothly, not constant motion. Use animation judiciously for critical alerts only (model goes red—brief flash to draw attention, then static red). Remember: radiators should enable ambient awareness, not demand constant attention. Subtle updates respected, aggressive animation resented.

⚠️ **Lack of Trust**: Radiator shows information that teams don't trust—known to be inaccurate, missing context, or conflicting with other sources. Team stops looking because radiator unreliable. Solution: Ensure accuracy—validate radiator metrics against source systems, document calculation methodology (how is this computed?), address discrepancies immediately, and maintain transparency about known limitations. Trust is earned through consistency and accuracy. One metric proven unreliable undermines trust in entire radiator. Fix accuracy issues immediately or remove unreliable metrics until fixed.

⚠️ **No Feedback Loop**: Radiator deployed once, never updated despite changing needs. Information that was critical becomes less relevant, missing information becomes critical, team provides feedback but nothing changes. Radiator becomes outdated and ignored. Solution: Regular feedback cycles—quarterly reviews with team discussing what's working, what's missing, what's unnecessary. Evolution based on usage patterns (analytics showing which metrics viewed most, which ignored). Radiators should evolve as team's work and priorities evolve. Static radiators become stale.

## Quick Decision Guide
**Use information radiators for:** Team coordination (shared awareness of status), operational monitoring (production system health), progress tracking (sprint/project visibility), issue awareness (incidents and alerts), resource management (capacity and utilization), any context where ambient awareness improves coordination and response speed

**Simpler approaches sufficient for:** Infrequent information needs (checked weekly or monthly), comprehensive analysis (requiring deep investigation), personal tracking (individual metrics not shared), low-change information (rarely updates), small teams with constant face-to-face communication (natural ambient awareness already exists)

**Information radiators critical when:** Managing complex distributed systems (need coordination), distributed teams (lack natural ambient awareness), rapid response required (issue detection speed matters), multiple concurrent activities (need shared priority understanding), stakeholder transparency important (external visibility required)
