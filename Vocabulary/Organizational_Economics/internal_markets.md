# Internal Markets

## One-Sentence Summary
Internal Markets are mechanisms where business units within organization buy and sell services from each other—using price signals and competition to drive efficiency.

## The Core Idea
- **Price signals:** Internal services have prices
- **Competition:** Units can choose internal or external service
- **Profit centers:** Internal units operate as profit centers
- **Efficiency:** Competition drives efficiency

## How It Works
1. **Define services:** What services do units provide?
2. **Set prices:** What do they cost?
3. **Enable choice:** Units can buy internally or externally
4. **Monitor:** Track service usage and pricing

## Think of It Like This
Internal markets are like **company services as internal marketplace**: Departments can buy HR services from HR department or external firm; pricing creates competition.

## The "So What?" Factor
- **Efficiency:** Competition drives cost reduction
- **Innovation:** Units innovate to stay competitive
- **Transparency:** Clear pricing shows cost/value
- **Fairness:** Price-based allocation

## Watch Out For
⚠️ **Over-competition:** Can destroy cooperation if units fight.
⚠️ **Price wars:** Units undercutting each other.
⚠️ **Gaming the system:** Units may game pricing to disadvantage competitors.
⚠️ **Loss of synergy:** Competition can reduce collaboration.

## Practical Checklist

Before implementing internal markets:
- [ ] Have you defined which services can be internal markets?
- [ ] Can you set fair market prices?
- [ ] Can business units buy external alternatives?
- [ ] Is price transparency maintained?
- [ ] Are internal units competitive on cost and quality?
- [ ] Will competition improve or harm organization?
- [ ] Can you prevent destructive competition?
- [ ] Are profit incentives aligned with organizational goals?
- [ ] Can you manage price manipulation?
- [ ] Will units have option to opt-out internally?
