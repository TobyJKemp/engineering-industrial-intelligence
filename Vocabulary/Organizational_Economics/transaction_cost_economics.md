# Transaction Cost Economics

## One-Sentence Summary
Transaction Cost Economics analyzes costs of conducting transactions—both within organization (coordination costs) and with market (transaction costs)—determining whether to conduct internally or externally.

## The Core Idea
- **Transaction costs:** Cost of market transactions
- **Coordination costs:** Cost of internal coordination
- **Trade-off:** Choose structure minimizing total costs
- **Boundaries:** Determines where organizational boundaries are

## How It Works
1. **Calculate internal cost:** Cost to do internally
2. **Calculate market cost:** Cost to buy from market
3. **Compare:** Which is cheaper?
4. **Decide:** Make or buy based on costs

## Think of It Like This
Transaction cost economics is like **make-or-buy decision**: Company deciding whether to make component or buy from supplier; cost comparison determines decision.

## The "So What?" Factor
- **Strategic:** Determines organizational boundaries
- **Efficiency:** Minimizes total costs
- **Flexibility:** As costs change, structure can change
- **Understanding:** Explains why organizations structured as they are

## Watch Out For
⚠️ **Hidden costs:** Costs often hidden; not fully counted.
⚠️ **Dynamic costs:** Costs change; decisions need refresh.
⚠️ **Coordination failure:** Underestimating coordination complexity.
⚠️ **Market failure:** External market may not work as expected.

## Practical Checklist

Before making make-or-buy decisions:
- [ ] Have you identified all internal coordination costs?
- [ ] Have you identified all external transaction costs?
- [ ] Are cost comparisons fair and complete?
- [ ] Have you considered asset specificity and switching costs?
- [ ] What happens if supplier disappears?
- [ ] What's cost of managing external relationship?
- [ ] Can you maintain capability optionality?
- [ ] Are strategic considerations factored in?
- [ ] When should this decision be revisited?
- [ ] Have you stress-tested assumptions?
